The Wire

Once upon a spreadsheet, a founder thought she owned 55% of her company. She'd been showing that number to investors for 18 months. Then three SAFEs converted, the real math came in, and she owned 31%. This is the story of how that happens, and how to make sure it doesn't happen to you.

The Deep Dive

Your Cap Table Is Lying to You

Let me tell you about something that happens more often than anyone in this industry wants to admit.

A founder builds something real. Gets traction. Raises a bit of money from people who believe in the vision. Then one day, usually on the worst possible day, someone runs the actual numbers and the story the spreadsheet has been telling turns out to be fiction.

I've watched this play out more times than I'd like. The details change every time. The plot never does.

Act 1: The beginning

Two friends start a company. They split the equity 50/50 because they're equal partners and that feels right. They set aside 10% for an option pool because someone told them they should.

Everyone's happy. The spreadsheet looks great. Life is good.

Act 2: The invisible guests

Over the next year, they raise $400K from three angel investors. One SAFE at a $3M cap. Another at $5M. A third at $4M with a 20% discount. Three separate conversations, three handshakes, three moments of celebration.

Here's the thing about SAFEs. They stand for Simple Agreement for Future Equity, which is one of the great marketing achievements of our time. Every word in that name is designed to make you feel comfortable. "Simple." "Future." "Equity." It all sounds so... manageable.

But SAFEs don't show up on your cap table. Not yet. They're promises. They're guests who've RSVP'd to the party but haven't arrived yet. So the spreadsheet still reads 45/45/10. The founders still tell everyone they own 90% of their company.

They believe it. Why wouldn't they? The spreadsheet says so.

Act 3: The reveal

A VC offers to lead a $2M round at $10M pre-money. Everyone celebrates the valuation. Champagne. LinkedIn posts. "Excited to announce..."

Then the lawyers run the conversion model. And the room goes quiet.

Not the angry kind of quiet. The kind where someone is staring at a spreadsheet and their face is doing maths their mouth doesn't want to say out loud. The kind of quiet where a founder realises that the number they've been telling themselves, the number they put on every investor update, the number that made them feel like they were winning... was never real.

Combined founder ownership: 52%. Down from the 90% they'd been telling themselves.

And here's what makes it worse: none of this was hidden. Nobody lied. Nobody acted in bad faith. The math was always going to land here. The founders just never asked the spreadsheet the right questions.

Three characters in every version of this story

I've sat across the table from enough founders to see the pattern. The details change. The cast is always the same.

The Invisible Guest. That's the SAFE. It lives outside your cap table like a houseguest who hasn't arrived yet but already has a key. You issue one, then another, then a third, and your spreadsheet keeps telling you a story that stopped being true months ago. It's like checking your bank balance without looking at the cheques you've written. The number on screen feels real. It isn't.

The Scenario Nobody Ran. I ask every founder the same question: "What does your cap table look like if you raise at $8M instead of $12M?" The usual answer is a long pause. Then "I'd have to ask my lawyer." That pause is the most expensive silence in startups. Your lawyer should have already shown you this movie at three different speeds. If they haven't, they're doing paperwork, not architecture.

The Shuffle. This one is subtle, and there's nothing malicious about it. When a VC says they want a 15% option pool "post-money," the standard practice is that the pool comes from the founders' shares, not from the new investment. The founders dilute themselves to create it. The VC's percentage is calculated after. It's perfectly standard. It's perfectly legal. And most founders don't fully understand the mechanic until after the champagne has gone flat and the documents are signed.

The toolkit that could have saved her

The founder I mentioned at the top? She called me on a Wednesday evening. By Friday we'd rebuilt her cap table model, run conversion scenarios at four valuations, and she walked into her next investor meeting knowing exactly where she stood. The round closed. She kept 48%. Not 55%, but a real number she understood and had negotiated for, not a fiction she'd inherited.

The difference wasn't intelligence. She's brilliant. The difference was that somebody finally showed her the movie before the ending.

Here's what she wished she'd had earlier:

A conversion model that breathes. Not a static spreadsheet. A model that takes every SAFE, every note, every promise, and shows you what happens at three different valuations. Your dream scenario. A realistic one. And the one that keeps you up at night. If you can see the range, you can negotiate inside it.

A running score. Every time you issue a new instrument, the model updates. If the next SAFE pushes your combined ownership below 50% at any reasonable valuation, that's a conversation worth having before you sign. Not after.

A question you're not afraid to ask. When a term sheet says "15% option pool," ask: pre-money or post-money? Who bears the dilution? Is the pool sized for your actual hiring plan or is it bigger than you need? These aren't adversarial questions. They're the questions that separate founders who know their own company from founders who think they do.

A tool that can't lie. Carta, Pulley, Ledgy, whatever works in your jurisdiction. Not a spreadsheet. A tool that models conversion scenarios and shows you the real numbers. Because spreadsheets don't update themselves, and the gap between what you think you own and what you actually own tends to grow in the dark.

The Blueprint

Cap Table Reality Check: 5 Questions

1. What's my fully diluted ownership? Not the number on your current spreadsheet. The number after every SAFE, note, and option converts.

2. At what valuation does my ownership drop below 50%? Model it. If that number is lower than your expected Series A, that's a conversation worth having this week.

3. Who has pro-rata rights? Every investor with pro-rata can maintain their percentage in your next round. More dilution pressure on you. Worth knowing.

4. When was the last time someone showed you a conversion waterfall? If the answer is "never," find a lawyer who thinks in pictures, not just documents.

5. Does your cap table tool match your legal documents? If the numbers tell different stories, one of them is wrong. Better to find out now.
