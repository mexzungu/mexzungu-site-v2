## Track 1: What Is Venture Architecture?

I made this term up. The concept is as old as business. But nobody had a good name for the thing that sits between "expensive lawyer who sends you a 40-page memo you'll never read" and "your mate who did a startup once and thinks he knows everything."

A venture architect designs the structural foundation of your company. Ownership, governance, capital architecture, regulatory positioning. The stuff that's invisible until it breaks — and when it breaks, it breaks everything.

**It's not legal advice.** I'm very clear about this. I don't draft your contracts. I design the architecture that tells your lawyers what the contracts need to say. Architect vs. structural engineer. Both essential. Not the same job.

**Why you should care right now:** Structural decisions cost $3K to get right at incorporation. $30K to fix at Series A. $300K to untangle at Series B. The cost curve is exponential and it never bends in your favour.

---

## Track 2: Where to Incorporate

You incorporated where it was convenient. Your co-founder lives in Lagos, so you set up in Nigeria. Your uncle's lawyer is in Nairobi, so Kenya it is. Someone on Twitter said Delaware, so you googled "how to incorporate in Delaware" and followed the first link.

None of those are structural decisions. They're vibes.

**Three questions that actually matter:**
1. Where do you operate? (You need a legal entity there.)
2. Where will your investors come from? (They want a jurisdiction they recognise.)
3. Where do you want to exit? (The end of the movie should inform the opening scene.)

**The rule of thumb:** If you're raising from US VCs → Delaware. Operating in Africa with DFI/impact money → UK or Mauritius holdco. Always have a local opco where the business actually runs. And don't incorporate in the Cayman Islands unless you have a real reason, because investors will assume the reason is "hiding something."

## Track 3: Cap Table 101

Your cap table is not a spreadsheet. It's a power map. Every line is a political relationship disguised as a number. And most founders have no idea theirs is broken until an investor's lawyer tells them the deal is dead.

**The hits:**
- **50/50 splits** = deadlock machine. Someone needs to be in charge.
- **No vesting** = your co-founder leaves after 3 months and keeps 30% forever. Good luck with that.
- **Phantom equity promises** = you told your first engineer they'd "get equity" over beers. No document. Now they want 5%.
- **Unmodelled SAFEs** = you think you own 80%. Your SAFEs convert and you own 45%. Surprise.

**The rule of thumb:** Vest everyone. Model everything. Write it down. If it's not documented, it didn't happen — until it happens in court, and then it really happened.

---

## Track 4: Governance

You think governance is bureaucracy. It's not. It's a decision-making architecture. And right now yours is "whoever shouts loudest on the WhatsApp group."

**The hits:**
- **Board control** is more important than valuation. A 10% better price means nothing if investors control whether you keep your job.
- **Reserved matters** are the fine print that runs your company. I've seen agreements where buying a $5K laptop needed board approval. That's not governance. That's a cage.
- **Drag-along rights** mean investors can force you to sell. You get your percentage, but zero say in the price, the buyer, or the timing.

**The rule of thumb:** There's no ethical non-monogamy in your duty of loyalty. When you're at the board table, you're committed to the company and the company only. And when you disagree with a decision, say "I vote against" and make sure it's in the minutes. Those might be the four most important words in corporate law.

---

## Track 5: Your First Fundraise

Fundraising is the moment every structural shortcut comes home to roost. Your product might be incredible. Your investors' lawyers don't care. They care about whether you actually own your IP, whether your cap table adds up, and whether your corporate documents look like they were prepared by adults.

**What investors' lawyers actually check:** Cap table, corporate docs, IP ownership, employment agreements, regulatory compliance. If any of these are messy, the deal either dies, reprices, or comes with conditions that cost you more than the money is worth.

**What to sign:** 1x non-participating liquidation preference, weighted average anti-dilution, standard pro-rata rights.

**What to walk away from:** Full ratchet anti-dilution, participating preferences, a board where investors control from day one. If a VC hands you a term sheet with participating preferences and full ratchet, what they're really handing you is a creative document about how they plan to own your company for less than it's worth. Smile, say thank you, walk out.

## Track 6: Five Bombs That Blow Up at Series A

1. **No vesting.** Ghost co-founder owns 25%. Investor walks.
2. **Messy cap table.** Three spreadsheets, none of them match. Due diligence dies.
3. **No IP assignment.** The contractor who built your MVP technically owns it. Investor panics.
4. **Wrong jurisdiction.** Restructuring mid-raise costs $85K and four months.
5. **Handshake governance.** Investor asks for 12 months of board minutes. You don't have any.

Every single one of these is preventable. Every single one costs 10x more to fix than to set up properly in the first place.

## Track 1: What Is Venture Architecture? (Extended Mix)

Growing up in Mexico, I learned something about buildings. In earthquake country, what matters isn't how beautiful the building looks. It's whether it's still standing after the ground shakes. The prettiest building in Mexico City means nothing if the foundation wasn't designed for the seismic zone it sits in.

Startups operate in a permanent earthquake zone. The market shifts. Co-founders leave. Investors change their minds. Regulations appear from nowhere. And the structure you set up in month one has to survive all of it — or everything you built on top of it cracks.

That's venture architecture. The seismic-proof foundation for companies that are moving too fast to think about foundations.

### What I actually do (and what I don't)

Let me be really specific about this, because the line matters.

A lawyer tells you what's legal. Important person. Respectable job. Very expensive shoes.

An accountant tells you what's tax-efficient. Also important. Less expensive shoes, usually.

A venture architect tells you what structure will actually work — for your specific company, your specific market, your specific level of ambition and chaos. We're the person who looks at the whole picture and says "if you incorporate here, raise money this way, and govern yourselves like that, you can scale to here without anything blowing up."

That means five things:

**Ownership and control design.** Here's a fun fact that ruins most founders' days: ownership and control are not the same thing. You can own 51% and still not control your company. I know, I know. It sounds like something a lawyer made up to bill more hours. It's not. If your shareholders' agreement gives your investors a veto on key decisions, you own the company on paper and they run it in practice. This is the most important structural decision you'll make, and you probably made it while you were also dealing with a product launch, a broken CI/CD pipeline, and the fact that your co-founder's laptop got stolen.

**Capital and financing architecture.** How you raise money is not just a business decision. It's a structural one. Every SAFE you sign, every convertible note you issue, every priced round you close — they all interact with each other on your cap table in ways that most founders never model. It's like adding instruments to a song without checking if they're in the same key. Each one sounds fine individually. Together they might be noise.

**Governance frameworks.** I'll save the full rant for Track 4. Short version: governance is not bureaucracy. Governance is "when we disagree, and we will disagree, who decides?" If your answer is "we'll figure it out" — congratulations, you've just described the governance framework of a group of friends planning a holiday. That works for Ibiza. It does not work for a company with investors, employees, and a burn rate.

**Regulatory positioning.** This one's less sexy but I've seen it kill companies. Where you incorporate, how you structure across jurisdictions, what licences you need. A fintech that doesn't think about regulatory positioning is a fintech that gets shut down by a regulator who doesn't care how cool your app is. I once worked with a company that was 3 months from launch when they discovered they needed a licence that takes 18 months to obtain. In a different jurisdiction. Where they'd need a local entity they didn't have. With capital requirements they couldn't meet. Structural planning would have caught this on day one.

**Transaction structuring.** The big moments: a fundraise, an acquisition offer, a strategic partnership, an exit. When these moments arrive, the structure you set up years ago determines how much money you make, how much control you keep, and whether the deal happens at all. I've watched founders leave millions on the table because their structure wasn't built for the transaction that showed up. That's not bad luck. That's bad architecture.

### The cost of "later"

I hear the same thing every week from founders: "We'll sort out the structure when we're bigger."

This is like saying "I'll install the plumbing after the building is finished." You can technically do it. You'll just have to tear up every floor in the building to run the pipes.

The cost of getting it right at incorporation: maybe $3,000.
The cost of fixing it at Series A: $30,000 and a lot of awkward conversations.
The cost of untangling it at Series B: $300,000, six months of legal work, and the distinct possibility that the round dies waiting.

I worked with a SaaS company that incorporated in their home country because — and I'm quoting here — "the lawyer was already there." Two years later, raising from US VCs, they had to do a full restructuring: new Delaware holdco, asset transfer, share swap, new shareholders' agreements for every existing investor. Four months. $85,000. The round almost died.

The lawyer was already there. $85,000.

## Track 2: Where to Incorporate (Extended Mix)

I've lived in six countries. I've incorporated entities in more jurisdictions than I can remember. I've filed paperwork in French, Spanish, English, and whatever language they speak in Delaware (it's a dialect of Pure Capital).

Here's what I know about jurisdiction: founders choose it the way most people choose a restaurant when they're starving. Whatever's closest. Whatever someone recommended. Whatever shows up first on Google.

And then, like that impulse restaurant choice, they spend the next two years regretting it.

### The three-question test

Every jurisdiction decision should pass through three questions. Get them right and you're fine. Get them wrong and you're restructuring mid-fundraise while your lead investor's patience slowly evaporates.

**Question 1: Where do you operate?**

If you have employees in Kenya, you need a Kenyan entity. Period. You can't run a Kenyan operation through a Delaware shell and pretend the employment law, tax obligations, and banking requirements of Kenya don't apply to you. They do. Local authorities don't care about your clever structure. They care about whether you're compliant in their jurisdiction.

**Question 2: Where will your investors come from?**

This is the one nobody thinks about until it's too late. Investors have preferences — strong ones. US VCs overwhelmingly prefer Delaware C-Corps because their lawyers know Delaware law, their fund documents are set up for Delaware investments, and the precedent is clear. Asking a Sand Hill Road VC to invest in a Kenyan limited company is like asking them to learn a new language mid-deal. They can do it. They won't want to. And the friction might kill your round.

Similarly, DFIs and impact investors working in Africa often prefer UK holdcos or Mauritius entities because they have treaty networks that make sense for the capital flows they're managing.

Your incorporation jurisdiction is a signal. It says "I'm set up for the kind of money I'm trying to raise." Or it says "I didn't think about this at all."

**Question 3: Where do you want to exit?**

If your big dream is an acquisition by Google, being a Delaware C-Corp makes that transaction dramatically simpler. Google's lawyers have done a thousand Delaware acquisitions. They've done very few Rwandan ones.

If your dream is listing on the Nairobi Securities Exchange or the Johannesburg Stock Exchange, you want your ultimate holding entity in a jurisdiction that makes that listing process smooth.

The end of the movie should inform the opening scene. Most founders write the opening scene without knowing what movie they're making. Then they're surprised when the ending doesn't work.

### The holdco / opco architecture

At some point, someone — probably an investor, probably during a call where you're trying to close a deal — will say "you need a holding company."

They're probably right. But let me explain what that actually means instead of leaving you to google it at midnight.

A **holdco** sits on top. It holds the shares, the IP, the investments. This is where your investors put their money.

An **opco** sits underneath. It operates the business in a specific market. Employees, revenue, expenses, all the messy real-world stuff.

It's like a restaurant franchise. There's a parent company (holdco) that owns the brand, the recipes, the trademarks. And there are individual restaurants (opcos) in different cities that actually cook the food and serve the customers. A kitchen fire in the Lagos restaurant doesn't burn down the whole franchise — the holdco is protected.

Three reasons to do this:
1. **Capital protection.** Investors' money sits in the holdco, not the entity getting sued.
2. **Multi-market operations.** One holdco, multiple opcos. Clean lines.
3. **Tax efficiency.** The right holdco jurisdiction can reduce friction on dividends, capital gains, and cross-border transfers. Legally. (Get professional advice. This is not the place to be creative.)

### How doing it wrong actually looks

I'm going to tell you a story that should make you slightly uncomfortable.

Three founders. University friends. Built an edtech product in their home country. Incorporated locally because the process took 48 hours and cost $200. Great.

They get traction. They get into an accelerator. The accelerator's demo day goes well. A US-based VC wants to lead their seed round.

"We love the product. We love the team. But we can't invest in a [home country] entity. Can you flip to Delaware?"

A "flip" is a restructuring where you create a new entity in a new jurisdiction and transfer everything — IP, contracts, shares, bank accounts — from the old one to the new one. It sounds simple. It is not simple.

This flip took them four months. $85,000 in legal and accounting fees. Two of their three SAFE investors had to be brought along with amended agreements. Their employment contracts had to be restructured. Their IP assignment had to be re-executed. Their bank account had to be moved. And the whole time, the VC's commitment was "soft" — no signed term sheet until the restructuring was complete.

For four months, they were in limbo. Building product, serving customers, and praying that the VC didn't find a different deal.

The incorporation cost $200. The fix cost $85,000. The risk was the entire company.

### What I tell people

- **Building for US VCs?** Start with Delaware. Templates are standardised. Lawyers are cheaper. Investors don't blink.

- **Operating in Africa with DFI/impact money?** UK or Mauritius holdco, local opcos. Well-understood by the investor class that matters.

- **Running a business in a specific country?** You need a local entity. Don't be clever. Be compliant.

- **Thinking about the Cayman Islands or BVI?** Only if you have a legitimate structural reason. "Tax optimisation" without substance is a red flag. Investors will ask. Regulators will look harder.

- **Already incorporated in the "wrong" place?** Don't panic, but don't wait either. Restructuring at pre-seed is a weekend project. Restructuring at Series A is a four-month ordeal. You choose.

## Track 3: Cap Table 101 (Extended Mix)

I wrote a whole essay about this — "Why Your Cap Table Is a Ticking Time Bomb" — and it's on our website if you want the full horror-movie version. But let me give you the director's commentary here.

A cap table is not a spreadsheet. I need you to really hear this, because everyone nods when I say it and then immediately goes back to treating it like a spreadsheet.

Your cap table is the constitution of your company. It determines who has power, who gets paid, who can block decisions, and who can force you out of the company you built. Every row is a relationship. Every percentage is a political statement. And most founders treat it with the same attention they give to updating their LinkedIn profile — which is to say, almost none until something goes wrong and suddenly it's very urgent.

### The conversation nobody wants to have

Two friends start a company. Day one. Full of excitement. They're going to change the world. They split the equity 50/50 because they're equal partners and equals share equally. It's only fair.

Here's what they've just done: they've built a company with no tiebreaker.

This works beautifully when you agree on everything. It works beautifully for about four months. Then one of you wants to pivot and the other doesn't. One of you wants to take investment and the other wants to bootstrap. One of you wants to hire that expensive engineer and the other thinks it's too early.

Now what? You're 50/50. Nobody can outvote anybody. Every disagreement is a hostage negotiation between people who are supposed to be building a product.

I once sat in a room — a beautiful office, expensive coffee, the works — watching two co-founders who'd been 50/50 for three years try to make a decision about hiring a CFO. One wanted an experienced hire at $180K. The other wanted a junior at $80K. They'd been arguing about it for six weeks. Six. Weeks. Meanwhile, their financial reporting was a disaster, their investors were losing confidence, and their competitors were not having six-week arguments about anything.

The solution was obvious: one of them needed to be the CEO with the final say. But by the time I got involved, neither would budge. Egos were in the room. The relationship was damaged. What should have been a 10-minute conversation in month one became a structural crisis in year three.

**The fix is almost embarrassingly simple:** Agree on who's the CEO. Give the CEO 51% or a casting vote. Write it down. Put it in the shareholders' agreement. Then go back to building your product.

If you can't agree on who's in charge, that's the most important piece of information you'll ever learn about your partnership. Better to learn it now than when there's $2M of investor money at stake and a board watching you fight.

### Vesting: the thing that will save your company (or the absence that will kill it)

I'm going to tell you about vesting the way I tell every founder, which is with the story of the Ghost.

The Ghost is a person who helped start the company. Maybe they wrote some code. Maybe they designed the logo. Maybe they just had the original idea over drinks one night. They got equity — sometimes a lot of equity — because at the time, everyone was excited and generous and nobody thought about what would happen if things changed.

Then things changed. The Ghost stopped showing up. Stopped contributing. Got a job somewhere else. Moved to another country. Became unreachable. But their shares? Still right there on the cap table. 20%. 25%. Sometimes more.

Now you're trying to raise money. The investor looks at the cap table and says: "Who's this person with 25%? What do they do?"

"They... were involved at the beginning."

"Are they involved now?"

"No."

"Do you have a buyback mechanism?"

"A what?"

"Do you have vesting?"

"..."

That silence is the sound of your round dying.

**Vesting means your shares are earned over time.** The standard: four years, one-year cliff. If you leave before a year, you get nothing. After a year, 25% vests. Then it accrues monthly or quarterly for the next three years. You stay four years, you earn everything. You leave early, the company gets the unvested shares back.

This is not about trust. I know you trust your co-founder. I know you're best friends. I know you went to university together and you'd never screw each other over.

That's beautiful. Put it in a shareholders' agreement.

Because trust isn't a corporate document. Trust doesn't hold up in court. Trust doesn't comfort an investor looking at a cap table with a phantom 25% shareholder who's been surfing in Bali for two years.

### SAFEs: the instrument everyone uses and nobody understands

SAFEs — Simple Agreements for Future Equity. The name alone is a masterclass in marketing. "Simple." "Future." "Equity." Every word is reassuring. None of them are accurate.

A SAFE is a bet. You're betting that your company will be worth more later, and in exchange for money now, you're giving the investor the right to convert into equity at a discount when that "later" arrives. The investor gets a good deal. You get cash without having to agree on a valuation today.

Sounds great. Here's the trap.

SAFEs are invisible. They don't show up on your cap table until they convert. So you issue one SAFE at a $3M cap. Then another at a $5M cap. Then a third at a $4M cap with a 20% discount and an MFN clause. Your spreadsheet still says you own 80% of the company.

You own nothing of the sort.

When those SAFEs convert at your Series A, the cap table reshuffles entirely. The founder I mentioned in the blog post? He thought he owned 55%. After conversion he owned 31%. He'd been giving away his company one SAFE at a time, one handshake at a time, and the spreadsheet he was looking at was a lie.

**How to not be that person:** Model every SAFE and convertible at multiple valuation scenarios. There are free tools for this. Use them. If you don't know how, hire someone for two hours to build you a model. If you can see what your cap table looks like at $5M, $10M, and $20M pre-money, you can make informed decisions. If you can't, you're flying blind with other people's money.

## Track 4: Governance (Extended Mix)

When I was in big law, governance was oxygen. You didn't think about it. It was just there. Board meetings happened. Minutes were taken. Authority lines were clear. Nobody had to ask "who makes this decision?" because the answer was always obvious and always documented.

Then I moved to startups.

Oh my god.

I once joined a call with three co-founders who hadn't had a formal board meeting in 14 months. They had investors. They had employees. They had customers in four countries. They were making decisions on WhatsApp. Not a dedicated channel, mind you. The same WhatsApp group where they also shared memes, birthday wishes, and links to apartments one of them was looking at in Cape Town.

I asked for their board minutes. They sent me a Google Doc called "DECISIONS (misc)" with 17 bullet points, three of which started with "we should probably..." and one that just said "talk to James about the thing."

That's not governance. That's a group chat with delusions of grandeur.

### Why this matters more than you think

Here's the thing about governance that nobody tells first-time founders: **it's not about following rules. It's about knowing who decides when you disagree.**

When you're two founders and you agree on everything, you don't need governance. You need a laptop and a dream. Beautiful. Inspiring. Enjoy it while it lasts.

The moment you take someone else's money, hire someone, sign a contract, or have a disagreement with your co-founder about anything more significant than lunch — you need governance. Not because the law says so (though it does). Because without it, you're one bad argument away from paralysis.

I'll put it in relationship terms because apparently that's my thing now. There's no ethical non-monogamy in governance. You can't have a board that's "sort of" in charge. You can't have decision-making authority that's "shared" in some undefined way. Shared authority without explicit rules is no authority at all. And no authority means that when the hard decisions show up — and they will, I promise they will — nobody can make them.

### The board: the maths that run your company

Your board is the highest authority in your company. Higher than the CEO. Higher than the founders. If your investors have a board seat, they have a direct say in the most important decisions your company will make — including whether you, the founder, keep your job.

Most founders negotiate valuation like their life depends on it and barely glance at board composition. This is backwards.

Here's the maths:

**Seed round:** 2 founder seats, 1 investor seat. You control the board 2-1. Good.

**Series A:** They want another seat. Now it's 2-2. You add an "independent" director as a tiebreaker. Who picks the independent? If the investor picks, it's really 2-3. If you pick, they'll push back. If you agree jointly, you'd better agree on someone genuinely independent.

**Series B:** Two investor groups now. They each want a seat. You're at 2-3. The independent is the swing vote on everything, including whether to fire you.

See how fast this moved? Three rounds and you went from owning the board to needing someone else's permission to run your company. That's not a bug. That's exactly how term sheets are designed. The valuation is the number they let you celebrate. The board composition is how they actually control the outcome.

**What I tell founders:** Negotiate board seats like they're shares. Because functionally, they are.

### Reserved matters: the clause that eats your freedom

Somewhere in your shareholders' agreement — probably around page 27, in a section nobody reads — there's a list called "reserved matters" or "protective provisions." This is a list of decisions you can't make without investor approval.

Normal reserved matters: issuing new shares, selling the company, taking on significant debt, changing the business plan. These are reasonable. Investors should have a say in existential decisions.

But I've seen lists that include: "any expenditure above $5,000." "Any new hire above $50,000 salary." "Any contract with a term longer than 12 months." "Any change to the company's website."

At that point, you don't run a company. You run a company-shaped permission request system. Every decision goes through a board that meets quarterly. Your competitors don't have this problem. Your competitors are moving.

**The fix:** Read the reserved matters list before you sign. Negotiate the thresholds up. Remove anything operational. Keep the existential stuff — share issuances, major debt, company sales, CEO changes. Cut the rest. If they won't budge, ask yourself whether this investor actually trusts you to run the company they're investing in. If the answer is no, that tells you everything you need to know about the relationship.

### The drag-along: the clause that makes you sell your company

I'll end this section with the one that catches everyone off guard.

**Drag-along rights** allow a supermajority of shareholders (usually 75%) to force all other shareholders to sell their shares on the same terms. Including you.

Here's the scenario: You own 30%. Your investors collectively own 70%. An acquirer shows up with an offer your investors like but you hate. Maybe the price is too low. Maybe you want to keep building. Doesn't matter. 70% triggers the drag-along. You're forced to sell.

You get your 30% of the proceeds, sure. But you had no say in whether to sell, who to sell to, what the terms were, or when it happened. Your company is gone. Thanks for playing.

**This is standard.** Every SHA has some version of this. The negotiation isn't about removing it — it's about the threshold. Is it 75%? 90%? Does it require a minimum price? Does the founder have a right of first refusal? These details are the difference between a fair provision and a loaded gun.

Read the drag-along before you sign. I can't stress this enough. I've watched founders discover their own drag-along clause for the first time when the acquirer's offer arrived. By then it was too late. The time to negotiate was at signing. That moment passed.

## Track 5: Your First Fundraise (Extended Mix)

Let me tell you what actually happens during a fundraise. Not the TechCrunch version where everything is exciting and founders ring bells. The real version.

You spend three months pitching. You get rejected 40 times. You have one VC who's interested. They send you a term sheet. You're so relieved that someone wants to give you money that you're ready to sign whatever they put in front of you.

Stop. Breathe. Read the document.

Your term sheet is not a love letter. It's a structural framework that will govern your company for years. And the VC has a team of lawyers who draft these for a living. You have... your co-founder's cousin who "did some contract law."

This is the moment where structural preparation pays off. Or where the absence of it costs you everything.

### What their lawyers actually look for

I'm going to let you in on something. Investor due diligence is not about your product. Their lawyers have no idea what your product does, and they don't care. What they care about is risk. Specifically, the risk that their client's money disappears into a structurally unsound company.

They will check:

1. **Cap table.** Does the ownership add up? Is every share properly issued? Are there phantom claims? Is there vesting?

2. **Corporate documents.** Do your articles match your SHA? Are your board minutes up to date? Have you actually held board meetings?

3. **IP ownership.** Does the company own its product? Did every founder, employee, and contractor sign an IP assignment? (The honest answer for most startups is "we think so." That's not good enough.)

4. **Employment and contractors.** Is everyone properly contracted? Are equity grants documented?

5. **Compliance.** Are you legal in every jurisdiction where you operate?

If any of this is messy, one of three things happens: the deal dies, the valuation drops, or the terms get worse. None of these are good for you.

### The term sheet decoder

**Liquidation preference.** Who gets paid first when the company is sold.

*How to do it right:* 1x non-participating. Investor gets their money back first, then everyone shares what's left. Fair. Standard.

*How to do it wrong:* 2x participating. Investor gets double their money back AND shares in the remaining proceeds. You just gave them two bites at the cherry and left yourself crumbs.

**Anti-dilution.** What happens to the investor's shares if you raise at a lower valuation next time.

*How to do it right:* Weighted average. The adjustment is proportional and reasonable.

*How to do it wrong:* Full ratchet. The investor's shares get repriced to the new lower price as if the original deal happened at that price. This is punitive. If someone offers you full ratchet, they're telling you they expect your company to go down in value. Think about what that means for the partnership.

**Board composition.** Covered in Track 4. Negotiate this as carefully as valuation. More carefully, actually.

### The sign / negotiate / walk test

**Sign it:** 1x non-participating preference, weighted average anti-dilution, standard pro-rata rights, reasonable information rights, 10-15% option pool.

**Negotiate it:** Board composition, reserved matters thresholds, founder vesting acceleration on exit, the exact scope of the option pool.

**Walk away from it:** Full ratchet, participating preferences above 1x, a board you don't control at seed, redemption rights (the investor can force you to buy back their shares — yes, this exists). If you see these terms and the VC won't budge, that's not an investor. That's a lender wearing an equity costume.

### The checklist

Before you talk to a single investor, be honest with yourself:

- [ ] Cap table is clean, single source of truth
- [ ] Vesting is in place for all founders
- [ ] Shareholders' agreement is signed
- [ ] IP assigned to the company (signed, not "we'll get to it")
- [ ] All team properly contracted
- [ ] Option pool exists or is ready to create
- [ ] Board minutes exist and are current
- [ ] Every convertible instrument modelled at conversion
- [ ] Compliant in every jurisdiction you operate

If you can check all of these, your due diligence will be boring. Boring due diligence is the best due diligence. It means the lawyers have nothing to flag, and the deal closes on schedule.

## Track 6: Five Bombs (Extended Mix)

Every year I see the same five structural problems blow up the same kind of companies. The details change — different founders, different markets, different products. The underlying mistakes are always the same.

Think of this as a setlist of greatest hits. Except these hits are terrible and you don't want to hear any of them live.

### Bomb 1: The Ghost

A co-founder leaves. No vesting. They keep everything. You can't get the shares back because there's no buyback clause, no vesting schedule, no mechanism of any kind. The Ghost is on a beach somewhere, technically owning 25% of a company they stopped thinking about nine months ago.

Your Series A investor looks at this and says: "Clean this up before we invest." Cleaning it up means finding the Ghost, negotiating with someone who has zero incentive to cooperate, and potentially litigating. Cost: $30K-100K. Time: 3-9 months. Outcome: uncertain.

**Prevention cost: $2,000 in legal fees to set up vesting at incorporation.**

### Bomb 2: The Spreadsheet of Lies

Three different cap table spreadsheets. The one the founders use. The one the accountant has. The one that was sent to the accelerator six months ago. None of them match. None of them include the two SAFEs that were issued over email last quarter.

The investor's lawyers ask for a fully diluted cap table. Nobody can produce one. The lawyers ask questions nobody can answer. Due diligence stops.

**Prevention cost: One afternoon updating a single document after every transaction.**

### Bomb 3: The Unowned Product

The company's product was built by three people: the CTO (co-founder), a freelance designer (friend of a friend), and a contract developer (found on Upwork). The CTO signed an IP assignment. The other two didn't. Nobody thought about it.

Now the investor asks: "Does the company own its IP?" And the honest answer is: "Probably? The CTO definitely assigned his. The other two... we'll check?"

"We'll check" is not an answer that gives lawyers confidence. Especially when the contract developer has since moved to another country and isn't responding to emails.

**Prevention cost: One template IP assignment agreement, signed before any work begins.**

### Bomb 4: The $85,000 Address Change

We covered this in Track 2. Incorporated in the wrong jurisdiction. Restructuring mid-raise. New holdco, asset transfer, share swap, consent from existing investors. Four months, $85K, and a lead investor whose patience runs out somewhere around month three.

**Prevention cost: $3K and one conversation with someone who understands jurisdiction strategy.**

### Bomb 5: The WhatsApp Board

No board meetings. No minutes. No documented decisions. The company has been running on WhatsApp, verbal agreements, and the assumption that everyone remembers what was decided last month.

The investor asks for board minutes. There are none. They ask who authorised the $50K contract with the landlord. Nobody knows. They ask whether the decision to pivot from B2C to B2B was a board decision or a founder decision. The answer is "it kind of evolved."

"It kind of evolved" is not corporate governance. It's jazz. Jazz is great in music. In corporate law, jazz means nobody is in control.

**Prevention cost: Zero. A Google Doc with the date, who was present, what was discussed, and what was decided. Monthly. Takes 20 minutes.**

## The Encore

You've made it to the end. Congratulations. You now know more about startup structure than most founders learn before their first crisis.

Here's the thing that ties it all together, and it's so simple it almost feels like a scam: **structure is not the enemy of speed.**

I know the startup mythology says otherwise. Move fast and break things. Iterate. Ship. Don't let process slow you down. And there's truth in that — for product. For code. For marketing experiments.

But for the foundation of your company? The ownership, the governance, the legal architecture? "Move fast and break things" is the rallying cry of people who've never had to explain to an investor why their cap table has a ghost shareholder and their corporate documents haven't been updated since 2023.

The fastest-scaling companies I've worked with — across Chadbourne, across Africa, across everything I've done since — are the best-structured ones. Not because structure makes you fast. But because structure removes the friction that makes you slow. Clean cap tables close faster. Clear governance decides faster. Proper documentation exits smoother.

> Chaos starts companies. Structure builds them.

That's not a motto. That's the operating manual.

If this guide made you realise something is off in your structure — good. That feeling is the most valuable thing you'll get from these pages. Because the alternative is not knowing until the bomb goes off.

Now go fix it. And if you want help, you know where to find me.
